### Paper 2.1
**Title:** Changes in retinal vessel oxygen saturation using automated retinal oximetry in patients with significant carotid stenosis
**Authors:** Author list **not verified on source** (Frontiers full-text and PMC both returned HTTP 403 today; *flagged*).
**Year:** 2025
**Venue:** *Frontiers in Neurology*, vol. 16 (2025)
**Link / DOI:** https://doi.org/10.3389/fneur.2025.1600749 ; PMC12527905 (https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12527905/) ; full text https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/neurology/articles/10.3389/fneur.2025.1600749/full
**Metadata verified:** Title, journal (*Frontiers in Neurology*), volume (16), year (2025), DOI (10.3389/fneur.2025.1600749), PMC ID (PMC12527905), study design (case-control, **74 eye–artery pairs in 37 patients with >50% carotid stenosis**, automated retinal oximetry of arterial + venous saturation and vessel diameter), and the headline finding cross-confirmed across the Frontiers listing and the PMC index. **Could NOT confirm on source (flagged):** full author list and exact per-vessel venous-saturation values (publisher/PMC pages 403).
**Relevance score:** 6 / 10
*(Human, clinical, and explicitly **venous**: it reports retinal venous sO₂ and the A–V oxygen-extraction difference as the primary readouts. Downgraded from higher because it is (a) **fundus-camera/automated-oximetry** of the retina — a Oxymap-class spectroscopic technique already well represented in spirit by the retinal thread — not a new modality, and (b) retinal **vessel-level** saturation rather than a named systemic vein or blood-gas-calibrated SvO₂.)*

**Novelty:** A clinical application of **non-invasive automated retinal oximetry** to the carotid-stenosis population, testing whether reduced upstream carotid perfusion measurably changes downstream retinal oxygen extraction. The retinal vessels are terminal branches of the carotid basin, so the study asks a clean physiological question: does significant (>50%) carotid stenosis raise retinal venous saturation / shrink the A–V difference (i.e., reduce oxygen extraction)? The reported answer is a **negative/weak** one — the relationship between retinal oxygen extraction and stenosis severity is **weak and heavily modified by individual comorbidities**, and paired bilateral testing (16 patients) showed **no significant A–V-difference change** between the stenotic and non-stenotic sides. The novelty is therefore as much methodological-honesty (a well-powered case-control test that fails to confirm a simple stenosis→retinal-venous-saturation effect) as it is a positive biomarker claim.

**Integration insights:** This is a useful, sobering counterweight to the repository's retina-as-proving-ground thesis. Prior retinal entries — SP-SFDI venous αSO₂ (2026-06-14 §2.1), the BMC macular venular oximetry study (2026-06-16 §2.1), and the Gu et al. vis-OCT disease finding (2026-06-18 §2.1) — all reported retinal *venous* saturation moving as a disease biomarker. This study uses the **A–V difference / oxygen-extraction-fraction** readout that the project treats as the physiologically meaningful venous endpoint, but finds it **confounded by comorbidity** in a real clinical cohort. The integration lesson for non-invasive optical venous oximetry is concrete: vessel-level venous-saturation endpoints are sensitive to systemic confounders, so any optical SvO₂ surrogate (retinal or peripheral) will need comorbidity-aware calibration and adequate per-subject controls — exactly the bilateral-paired design used here. It also reinforces that **fundus spectroscopic oximetry** sits alongside vis-OCT, SP-SFDI and HSI as one of the small set of human-translatable optical methods that actually resolve a venous vessel, even though none yet delivers a blood-gas-calibrated systemic SvO₂. Honest caveats: retinal vessel-level, fundus-camera technique (not a novel modality), author list unverified on source, and a predominantly negative result.

## Cross-Topic Synthesis

### What this scan actually establishes
On a **one-day cadence** in a low-volume niche, the venous-specific non-invasive oxygen literature **again produced no brand-new publication** — the correct and expected outcome, now observed for the fifth consecutive short-cycle scan, and itself a signal worth recording. The value added this cycle is **one new clinical data point in the retinal optical-oximetry thread**, plus two new trial/context leads:

1. **A clinical retinal *venous* oximetry result enters the record (Paper 2.1) — with a cautionary, mostly-negative finding.** Frontiers in Neurology's carotid-stenosis study uses the A–V oxygen-extraction readout the project treats as the meaningful venous endpoint, but finds it **weak and comorbidity-confounded**, with no significant stenotic-vs-non-stenotic A–V difference on paired testing. This tempers the otherwise uniformly positive retinal-venous-biomarker signal from SP-SFDI, BMC macular oximetry, and Gu et al. vis-OCT: vessel-level venous saturation is real but noisy in real patients, and any optical SvO₂ surrogate will need comorbidity-aware calibration.
2. **A new venous-specific monitoring trial appears (NCT06511999).** Continuous jugular SjO₂ after cardiac arrest (U. Pittsburgh, recruiting, completion 2027) is the one *new* forward motion on Topic 1 — but it is a trial, not a paper, joining the standing hepatic-vein NIRS trial (NCT01891188) and the MRI-reference ScvO₂ validation trial (NCT05161884).
3. **No new modality breakthrough.** Today's photoacoustic/HSI/vis-OCT hits all map to already-catalogued work (Hybrid-Net = arXiv:2512.15394; sulfate phantoms = arXiv:2512.01458; IJV-PA = arXiv:2303.10775; Gu et al.; the HSI retina system). NIR-II PA and the Graefe's review are carried as technique/context leads.

### Persisting gaps (carried forward, unchanged and still open)
- **No new in-vivo human venous-PA sO₂ result.** The 2023 IJV-PA proof-of-concept (arXiv:2303.10775) remains the only direct human named-vein photoacoustic oximetry result; clinical-scale validation still does not exist.
- **The MRI validation-scale gap still dominates.** Two MRI venous-SvO₂ methods (T₂ iSvO₂; susceptometry) are blood-gas-validated at cohort scale, and an MRI-reference ScvO₂ validation trial is registered; no optical method approaches that bar.
- **Vessel-specific venous separation remains proven mainly in the retina (and thin/murine tissue).** Paper 2.1 adds a *human clinical* retinal venous data point — but it is fundus-camera vessel-level oximetry, and its main result is that the venous/A–V signal is comorbidity-confounded. Extension of single-vessel venous separation to an accessible systemic vein in thick human tissue is still unshown.
- **Comorbidity confounding is now an explicitly documented obstacle.** Paper 2.1 turns a previously implicit caveat into a measured finding: retinal venous-saturation/A–V endpoints did not track stenosis severity cleanly because of individual comorbidities — a direct warning for any optical venous-oximetry biomarker program.
- **Topic 1's only forward motion is trials, not papers.** Three venous-relevant trials are now tracked (hepatic-vein NIRS, post-arrest jugular SjO₂, MRI-reference ScvO₂); none has a results publication.

### Bottom line
A near-**empty cycle for new publications**, used to add one honest clinical retinal-venous-oximetry data point (Frontiers carotid-stenosis study, Paper 2.1) and two new trial/context leads. The new paper is genuinely venous in its readout but reports a **mostly-negative, comorbidity-confounded** result — a useful corrective rather than a breakthrough. Nothing this cycle alters the standing picture: **MRI is the absolute-reference calibrator, the retina is the proving ground (and now a documented cautionary case for confounding), the venous-occlusion test is the practical surrogate, and human deep-vein optical sO₂ at clinical scale remains the unfilled prize.** No arterial-only padding was added to reach five entries per topic, per the scan's honesty mandate.
